In the Attic

Władysław Szpilman

Survivor Testimony · Adapted from The Pianist

About the Chapter

This survivor testimony belongs to Unit 1, Trials and Triumphs. It records Władysław Szpilman's real experience of hiding from the Nazis in war-torn Warsaw during World War II, and how an unexpected act of humanity from a German officer, Wilm Hosenfeld, kept him alive.

Szpilman, a Polish Jew, was forced into the Warsaw ghetto, where he witnessed the horrors of the Holocaust. In hiding, starving and in despair, he is discovered by a German officer — but instead of being harmed, he is protected, fed, and encouraged to hold on until the war ends.

About the Author

Władysław Szpilman (1911–2000) was a Polish pianist and classical composer. His memoir The Pianist was published in 1946 and later adapted into an award-winning film by Roman Polanski in 2002. Szpilman composed over 500 works, including piano pieces, songs, and film scores.

A Note on the German Officer

Wilm Hosenfeld, the soldier who saved Szpilman, also saved many other individuals during the war. He was captured by the Red Army towards its end, could not contact Szpilman again, and died in a Soviet prison in 1952.

Summary (English)

Hiding among the ruins of a bombed building in Warsaw, Szpilman is discovered by a German officer. Terrified, he refuses to move, but the officer reassures him he means no harm and asks what he does for a living. Learning that Szpilman is a pianist, the officer leads him to a room with a piano and asks him to play.

With stiff, trembling fingers — unpracticed for over two years — Szpilman plays Chopin's Nocturne in C sharp minor. The officer listens in silence, then urges him to leave the city for safety. When Szpilman refuses, insisting he cannot leave, the officer realises he is hiding because he is Jewish. Rather than turning him in, the officer inspects Szpilman's hiding place, finds a hidden loft above the attic, and advises him to hide there instead. He promises to bring food.

Over the following weeks, the officer returns periodically with bread and supplies, encouraging Szpilman to "hang on," insisting the war will end soon. Szpilman endures freezing cold, isolation, and the constant fear of discovery, surviving on hope and the officer's occasional visits. On his last visit, the officer brings extra food and a warm eiderdown, revealing he must leave with his unit — but not before Szpilman insists on giving him his name, in case he can ever return the favour.

Eventually, Warsaw is liberated. Szpilman nearly loses his life again — this time almost shot by Polish soldiers who mistake him for a German because of the coat the officer had given him. Once they realise he is Polish, he is cared for, and finally walks free through the ruined streets of Warsaw for the first time in six years.

Theme: The story is a real, first-hand testimony to how compassion and humanity can survive even in the most brutal circumstances — and how one act of kindness, regardless of the side of a war someone is on, can save a life.

While We Read — Q & A

a. What was the officer's attitude to Szpilman?
He was calm, reassuring, and non-threatening — he made it clear from the start that he had no intention of harming Szpilman.
b. Why do you think the officer asked Szpilman to play the piano?
He was curious about Szpilman's profession and perhaps wanted a moment of humanity and beauty amid the surrounding devastation of war.
c. Why was Szpilman hesitant to play the piano?
He feared that if anyone heard the piano being played, his hiding place would be discovered and he would be caught.
d. How does the officer help Szpilman?
He finds a safer hiding spot (the loft above the attic), brings him food regularly, gives him a warm eiderdown and coat, and encourages him to keep hope alive.
e. What was the officer ashamed of?
He was ashamed of being German, given everything the Nazis had done during the war.
f. "Just hang on a few more weeks — the war will be over by spring at the latest." What is surprising about this statement?
It is surprising that a German officer is expressing hope for Germany's defeat and offering comfort to a Jewish fugitive — the opposite of what his uniform would suggest.
g. What does the conversation between the officer and Szpilman reveal about the bond between them?
It reveals mutual trust and quiet respect that transcended their opposing sides in the war — built through shared risk and honesty.
h. Why was there less artillery fire after a few weeks?
It suggests the war was gradually shifting, with fighting moving away or slowing down, hinting that liberation was approaching.
i. Why does Szpilman say that his hiding place was well chosen?
Because no one ever thought to search the hidden loft above the attic — it kept him undiscovered for a long time.
j. What do you think of Szpilman's advice to the German officer?
It shows deep gratitude and a genuine wish to help in return — offering his name so the officer could reach him if he ever needed help, despite Szpilman's own desperate situation.
k. What sounds did Szpilman hear from the attic?
Artillery fire, soldiers' footsteps, rustling rats and mice, and eventually radio announcements of Germany's defeat.
l. Why does Szpilman say that he had to be alone if he wanted to live?
Because being with others increased the risk of discovery — solitude, though painful, was necessary for survival.
m. What were the radio announcements about?
They announced the defeat of Germany and the liberation of Warsaw.
n. How does Szpilman survive the freezing cold?
With the eiderdown and the German military overcoat left for him by the officer.
o. What information did Szpilman hope to gather?
He wanted to know whether Warsaw had truly been liberated and whether it was safe to come out of hiding.
p. Why does the woman cry out when she sees Szpilman?
She mistook him for a German soldier because of the military coat he was wearing, and cried out in fear/warning.
q. What is the absurd situation Szpilman found himself in?
Having survived years of Nazi occupation as a hidden Jew, he now risked being shot by Polish soldiers — his own countrymen — in newly liberated Warsaw, mistaken for a German.

Let's Rewind

1. Why does the German officer help Szpilman and save his life?
Moved by compassion and remorse over the war, and perhaps by the shared humanity revealed through music, he chooses conscience over duty.
2. How does Szpilman maintain hope in extreme hardship and danger?
Through the officer's encouragement, small acts of kindness, and his own will to survive and reunite with a sense of normal life.
3. Why was Szpilman afraid of being killed by Polish soldiers, his own countrymen?
Because he was wearing the German coat given to him, making him look like an enemy soldier to the very people who had just been liberated.
4. How do luck, chance and human kindness help Szpilman to survive?
Chance led the officer to discover him rather than someone crueler; luck kept his hiding place unnoticed; kindness from a stranger on the "enemy" side provided food and hope.
5. Was it risky for the German officer to help Szpilman? Why?
Yes — helping or hiding a Jewish person was a crime under Nazi rule, and discovery could have cost the officer his own life or freedom.
6. Why is Szpilman's memoir classified as 'survivor testimony'? How does it enrich our understanding of the world?
It is a first-hand account of surviving genocide and war, told from lived experience rather than fiction. Such testimonies preserve historical truth, humanise statistics of tragedy, and remind readers of the value of compassion and resilience.
7–9. On the expression 'en masse'
'En masse' means "all together, as a group" — borrowed from French. It's similar to other borrowed phrases used in English (like "déjà vu" or "vice versa" from Latin), differing from native English idioms in that they retain their original foreign spelling and pronunciation.
