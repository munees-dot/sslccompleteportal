A Very Old Man with Enormous Wings

Gabriel García Márquez

Short story · Magical Realism

← Back to English

About the Chapter

This story opens Unit 1, Trials and Triumphs, whose theme is compassion, kindness, and resilience amid hardship. It uses magical realism — a style in which fantastical events are described in a completely matter-of-fact, everyday tone — to hold up a mirror to human indifference and exploitation.

A very old man with enormous wings falls into Pelayo's courtyard during a storm. Instead of being cared for, he is caged, put on display for money, and treated as a curiosity — until, one ordinary morning, he simply flies away.

About the Author

Gabriel García Márquez (1927–2014) was a Colombian novelist, one of the most celebrated writers of the 20th century and a pioneer of magical realism. He won the Nobel Prize for Literature in 1982. His best-known works include One Hundred Years of Solitude, Love in the Time of Cholera, and No One Writes to the Colonel.

Key Vocabulary Note

Magical realism blurs the line between fantasy and reality, much like a fairy tale — but the "magic" (an angel, a spider-woman) is treated as an ordinary, almost inconvenient fact of life rather than a miracle.

Summary (English)

During days of heavy rain, Pelayo finds a strange old man lying face down in the mud of his courtyard — a man with enormous, dirty wings tangled in filth. He and his wife Elisenda are baffled by the creature, who speaks in an unintelligible dialect. A neighbour who claims to know everything about life and death takes one look and declares him to be an angel, fallen from the sky, likely on his way to take their sick child.

Rather than caring for him, the couple locks the old man in the chicken coop. Word spreads quickly, and crowds gather to gawk at him, poking, plucking his feathers, and even branding him with a hot iron to see if he will react. Sensing an opportunity, Pelayo and Elisenda begin charging an entrance fee, and soon grow wealthy from the endless stream of curious visitors and desperate invalids hoping for a miracle cure.

Through it all, the winged old man endures every indignity with strange, silent patience — his only apparent "supernatural" trait. Their sick child recovers and grows up alongside the deteriorating captive, largely ignoring him once a travelling show brings a woman-turned-spider to town as a more fashionable attraction. The old man grows weaker, nearly dies over a hard winter, but survives — and slowly, unnoticed, new feathers begin to grow.

One morning, Elisenda finds him clumsily attempting to fly from the courtyard. After several failed, undignified attempts, he finally lifts off and disappears over the horizon. Elisenda watches him go with quiet relief — he had become, by then, not a wonder but a mere "annoyance in her life."

Theme: The story is a gentle, ironic critique of human indifference, greed, and the failure of compassion toward those who are old, weak, or simply different — even when they may be extraordinary.

What is Magical Realism?

A narrative style, closely associated with Latin American writers like García Márquez (and also Salman Rushdie, Angela Carter), where fantastical or magical events are presented within an otherwise realistic, everyday setting — described in the same matter-of-fact tone as ordinary events. Like a fairy tale, it blurs the line between fantasy and reality.

While We Read — Q & A

a. Why does the sight of the old man frighten Pelayo?
He is unfamiliar and strange — a man with enormous wings lying face down in the mud, groaning, is something Pelayo has never encountered.
b. Why was the old man unable to get up?
His enormous wings were impeded/tangled in the mud, making it impossible for him to rise despite his efforts.
c. Why do the couple consider the old man a "lonely castaway"?
He spoke in an incomprehensible dialect with a sailor's voice, so they assumed he was a shipwrecked sailor from a foreign ship.
d. Why was the neighbour woman sure that the old man was an angel?
She claimed to know everything about life and death, and declared at a glance that he was an angel who had come for the sick child but was too old and weak from the rain to succeed.
e. Why did Pelayo arm himself with a club?
He kept watch over the "captive angel" out of caution/fear, in case the mysterious being posed a threat.
f. What made the couple suddenly decide to treat the man with some kindness?
Their sick child recovered and woke up with an appetite that same night, which they attributed to the old man's presence, making them feel grateful ("magnanimous").
g. Why does Pelayo lock the old man in the chicken coop?
He did not know what else to do with the strange, captive being, so he treated him like livestock rather than a person.
h. Why do people treat the old man like a "circus animal"?
Crowds gathered to gawk at him, tossed food through the coop's openings, and treated the spectacle as entertainment rather than recognising his dignity.
i. Do you think Elisenda is shrewd and clever? Why?
Yes — she quickly turns the chaotic crowd into a business opportunity by fencing the yard and charging an admission fee, showing practical, business-minded thinking.
j. "His only supernatural virtue seemed to be patience." Why does the author say so?
Despite being pecked, having his feathers pulled, and being pelted with stones, the old man never reacted or resisted — an almost impossible calm that seemed his only remarkable, "angelic" quality.
k. Why do more people flock to see the woman turned into a spider rather than the old man?
Her show was cheaper to see, allowed visitors to ask questions about her "absurd state," and offered fresher, more sensational novelty than the now-familiar old man.
l. Why is the doctor surprised by the "logic" of the wings?
The wings seemed so naturally suited to the old man's completely human body/organism that the doctor couldn't understand why every human being didn't have them too.
m. Why does Elisenda let out a sigh of relief?
Watching the old man finally fly away and disappear meant she was free of the burden and "annoyance" he had become in her life.
